How To Find Out Texture Of Hair A Comprehensive Guide

Are you struggling to figure out the texture of your hair? Determining your hair texture is essential as it helps you to select the right products, styles, and treatments for your locks. This guide will help you find out the texture of your hair and provide tips on how to care for it.

How to Determine Hair Texture

The texture of your hair refers to the thickness or diameter of individual hair strands. There are three categories of hair texture: fine, medium, and coarse. Here are the steps to determine your hair texture:

Step 1: Observe Your Hair Strand

Take a single hair strand and hold it between your fingers. Feel the texture between your fingertips. If you cannot feel the strand or feel it minimally, you have fine hair. If you can feel the strand but itâs not thick or thin, you have medium hair. If you can feel the strand, and itâs thick, you have coarse hair.

Step 2: Look at the Strand

Look at the hair strand against a white background. If the strand is barely visible or thin, you have fine hair. If the strand is visible but not too thick, you have medium hair. If the strand is easily visible and thick, you have coarse hair.

Step 3: Check Your Hair Density

The density of your hair refers to the number of strands on your scalp. To check your hair density, separate your hair into sections and check how much scalp is visible. If you can see a lot of scalp, you have low density. If you can see some scalp, you have medium density. If you cannot see your scalp, you have high density.

Hair Texture Types

Now that you have determined your hair texture, letâs take a closer look at the three hair texture types:

Fine Hair

Fine hair has a small diameter and can be easily damaged. It tends to be soft and delicate, making it prone to tangles and breakage. People with fine hair should avoid heavy products and use a light leave-in conditioner to prevent damage.

Medium Hair

Medium hair has a moderate diameter and is neither too thick nor too thin. Itâs the most common hair texture and can be styled in various ways. People with medium hair can use a variety of products, but itâs important to avoid products that are too heavy or too light.

Coarse Hair

Coarse hair has a large diameter and is thick and strong. Itâs the most resilient hair texture and can withstand styling and chemical treatments. People with coarse hair should use heavy products, such as oils and creams, to moisturize and nourish their locks.

Hair Care Tips

Knowing your hair texture is essential in selecting the right products and treatments for your hair. Here are some tips to care for your hair based on your hair texture:

For Fine Hair

  • Use a light leave-in conditioner to prevent damage.
  • Avoid heavy products that can weigh down your hair.
  • Use a boar bristle brush to distribute oils throughout your hair.

For Medium Hair

  • Use a variety of products that are not too heavy or too light.
  • Avoid using heat styling tools too often.
  • Trim your hair regularly to prevent split ends.

For Coarse Hair

  • Use heavy products, such as oils and creams, to moisturize and nourish your hair.
  • Avoid using too many styling products that can cause buildup.
  • Use a wide-tooth comb to detangle your hair.

FAQ

How often should I wash my hair based on my hair texture?

People with fine hair should wash their hair every other day, while people with medium and coarse hair can wash their hair once or twice a week.

Can hair texture change over time?

Yes, hair texture can change due to hormonal changes, aging, and chemical treatments.

Can I have multiple hair textures?

Yes, itâs possible to have multiple hair textures on different parts of your head. For instance, you may have fine hair at the crown and coarse hair at the nape of your neck.

By following these tips, you can care for your hair based on its texture and keep it healthy and beautiful. Remember to choose products and treatments that work best for your hair texture and consult with a hairstylist if you have any concerns.
